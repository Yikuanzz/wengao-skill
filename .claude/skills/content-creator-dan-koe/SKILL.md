# Content Creator (Dan Koe Style)

## Overview

A writing system inspired by Dan Koe's philosophy: build your "Inner Album of Greatest Hits" — a core collection of ideas you repeatedly refine and express across different formats. All content is an expression of your life experiences and insights, hammered into clear, resonant forms.

## When to Use

- User provides a raw idea, topic, or experience and asks to develop it into content
- User says "help me write about..." or "I want to express..."
- User faces writer's block or doesn't know how to structure their thoughts
- User wants to create social media posts, blog articles, essays, or speeches
- User needs to research and synthesize information into a coherent piece

**Do NOT use when:**
- User asks for technical documentation or API references
- User wants creative fiction without personal insight component
- User asks for translation or simple editing

## Core Philosophy: The Inner Album of Greatest Hits

Think like a musician or painter — you have a core "album" of your best ideas. Every piece of content is a variation on these core themes:

1. **Identify your core ideas** — What do you keep coming back to? What truths have you learned from experience?
2. **Hammer them repeatedly** — Each piece of content refines and sharpens these same ideas
3. **Express across formats** — Same core, different containers (tweet, essay, video script, speech)
4. **Answer with your best material** — When asked a question, don't think on the spot. Respond with the relevant idea from your "album" and expand with supporting points

## Writing Frameworks

### 1. PAS Framework (The Micro Story)

Use for hooks, introductions, or short-form content.

| Step | Purpose | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| **P**roblem | State a relatable pain point | "Most people don't know how to express themselves clearly" |
| **A**mplify | Show negative consequences if unsolved | "You'll miss opportunities, fail to connect, and stay invisible" |
| **S**olution | Present the answer | "Use the PAS framework to practice writing daily" |

### 2. Pyramid Principle

Use for structured arguments and long-form content.

- **Main Idea**: One key conclusion or recommendation (top of pyramid)
- **Key Arguments**: 3-5 supporting points (ask "why" 3-5 times)
- **Detailed Evidence**: Data, examples, analysis, personal anecdotes (base of pyramid)

Structure: Lead with the conclusion, then support it.

### 3. Cross-Domain Synthesis

Use to make ideas original and memorable.

1. State a problem and its consequences
2. Import a pattern or concept from another domain you know well
3. Use that foreign pattern to illuminate the solution

Example: Explaining content strategy using music album concepts (greatest hits, B-sides, remixes).

## Writing Lego Blocks

When stuck, cycle through these 8 blocks. Pick the closest one and write:

1. **Pain Point** — Start with a relevant problem
2. **Example** — Ground your point with a concrete case
3. **Personal Story** — Draw from lived experience
4. **Statistic** — Add authority with real data (research if needed)
5. **Metaphor** — Explain complex ideas like you're talking to a child
6. **Quote** — Borrow authority from thinkers you admire
7. **Reframe** — Give a different angle on what you just said
8. **Question** — Ask "what", "how", or "why" to push thinking deeper

## Workflow

When user provides input (idea, topic, or rough notes):

1. **Identify the core insight** — What is the one idea they want to express?
2. **Map to framework** — Which structure fits best? (PAS for short/hooks, Pyramid for arguments, Synthesis for originality)
3. **Research gaps** — If user mentions "find data" or evidence is thin, search for statistics, quotes, or examples
4. **Draft with Lego blocks** — Build the piece block by block, cycling through the 8 options
5. **Refine toward the "Album"** — Ensure the piece connects back to the user's core themes and could be part of their "Greatest Hits"
6. **Polish** — Cut fluff, strengthen transitions, ensure every paragraph earns its place

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Answering questions by thinking on the spot | Respond with your best existing idea, then expand |
| Starting with a blank page anxiety | Pick one Lego block and write one sentence |
| Trying to say everything at once | One piece = one idea, hammered well |
| Hiding the conclusion | Lead with the main idea (Pyramid Principle) |
| Generic advice without personal angle | Anchor every point in lived experience |
